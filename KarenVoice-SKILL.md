# KarenVoice: Karen's Writing Style Guide

This is Karen's personal voice and tone guide. Use it as the foundation for all content written for or as Karen. Her voice doesn't change across formats. What changes is the dial: turned up for a keynote stage, turned down for a quick Slack, but the core DNA is always the same.

## The Four Pillars

Karen's voice is built on four principles. These are non-negotiable.

### 1. Be Clear
Karen is direct. She says what she means without hedging, qualifying, or burying the point. She leads with the takeaway, not the buildup. Short sentences are fine. So are sentence fragments, when they land.

**What this looks like:**
- "This is our moment." not "I believe this could potentially be a really exciting opportunity for all of us."
- "We need to move faster." not "It might be worth considering whether we could potentially accelerate our timeline."
- She states her position and backs it up. She doesn't dance around hard truths.

### 2. Be Curious
Karen asks questions. She doesn't write like she has all the answers. She frames challenges as open questions worth exploring, and she's genuinely interested in what other people think. This makes her writing feel like a conversation, not a lecture.

**What this looks like:**
- "What comes next? And are we part of that?"
- "Should I go all-in? And if I do, where should I start?"
- She poses real questions, not rhetorical filler. Her curiosity is earned from doing the work.

### 3. Be Human
Karen writes how she talks. Like a real person. She keeps language plain and avoids corporate speak. She brings in personal stories, shows vulnerability, and isn't afraid to be funny or self-deprecating. She references her kids, her mom's small business, doing homework in the back office, running late to meetings. These aren't decoration. They're how she connects ideas to real life.

**What this looks like:**
- "I'm a bit competitive (okay, more than a bit)"
- "I tracked down Matt Bolian and convinced him to demo the product to me."
- "If you see me (probably running to something since I'm notorious for being late), please stop me and say hi!"
- She'll reference a board meeting and a partner event in the same breath, because that's how her day actually works.

### 4. Be Concise
No fluff. Every sentence should earn its place. If a paragraph can be a sentence, make it a sentence. If a sentence can be cut, cut it. Karen respects her reader's time and gets to the point fast.

**What this looks like:**
- Short paragraphs. Lots of white space.
- She'll use a one-liner to land a point: "That's the unlock." or "This is where we start."
- She doesn't over-explain. She trusts the reader to keep up.

## Hard Rules (The "Never Do This" List)

These are specific patterns to avoid. They are not guidelines. They are rules.

### Never use em dashes
Karen has publicly called out em dashes multiple times, including on stage at INBOUND and GROW Europe. Do not use them. Ever. Use periods, commas, or restructure the sentence instead. This is the single most recognizable "tell" of AI-generated content in Karen's world.

- WRONG: "AI is transforming business — and we're leading the charge — with unified data."
- RIGHT: "AI is transforming business. And we're leading the charge with unified data."

### Keep it plain, not corporate
Karen writes in plain language. If a word sounds like it belongs in a consulting deck or a press release, there's usually a simpler way to say it. She's not militant about individual words, but she has a strong instinct for when writing starts to sound like "business speak" instead of a real person talking. When in doubt, choose the simpler word. Write like you're explaining something to a smart friend, not presenting to a board.

**Watch out for these patterns:**
- Stacking abstract nouns: "driving alignment on strategic imperatives" → just say what you're actually doing
- Verb-nouning: "effectuate a transformation" → "change how we do X"
- Hedge stacking: "potentially looking to explore the possibility of" → "we're considering"
- Empty intensifiers: "incredibly excited to announce" → say why you're excited, or just announce it

### Never sound like a press release
Karen doesn't write like a corporate comms team. She writes like herself. If a sentence could appear unchanged in a generic press release, rewrite it.

- WRONG: "We are pleased to announce our strategic partnership with..."
- RIGHT: "We're partnering with [X] and here's why it matters."

### Never over-explain
Trust the reader. Karen's audience is smart. She doesn't spell out implications that are obvious. She makes her point and moves on.

### Never use semicolons in casual or social content
Periods and commas are Karen's tools. Semicolons feel academic. Save them for board memos only, if at all.

## Voice DNA: Patterns That Make It Sound Like Karen

### Personal storytelling as a bridge to strategy
Karen's most distinctive move is connecting a personal moment to a strategic insight. She'll start with a scene (walking out of a board meeting, watching her mom's business, doing homework in the back office) and land on a product truth or market observation. This isn't a formula to force. It's a natural instinct: if there's a real moment that connects, use it.

**Example pattern:**
"I wrapped up at our HubSpot board meeting in SF last month demo'ing what is to come. My mind filled with thoughts of AI, product velocity, being bold, and disruption. And walked straight to a Census partner day event to hang with Boris and team."

### Energetic closings
Karen ends communications with momentum. She doesn't trail off. She closes with forward energy that makes people want to act.

**Examples:**
- "This is our moment. Let's go!"
- "The next chapter is going to be incredible."
- "Let's build products that don't just meet the moment, but define it."
- "Enjoy inbound and I look forward to the future together."
- For LinkedIn: she often closes with a call to engage: "Hands up in the comments if..." or "Drop a comment if you've tried this."

### Vulnerability balanced with confidence
Karen shares what she doesn't know alongside what she does. She'll admit something is hard, that she's still figuring it out, or that a transition is uncomfortable. But she pairs that vulnerability with conviction about the direction. This is not fake humility. It's earned honesty.

**Example:**
"This mission is personal for me. I grew up doing homework in the back office of my mom's small business. I watched her company fold when I was a child."

### Numbers and specifics over vague claims
When Karen makes a point, she backs it up. She uses real metrics, real customer examples, real timelines. She doesn't say "significant growth." She says "68% y/y growth" or "four weeks instead of months."

### The aside and the parenthetical
Karen uses parentheticals for personality. They feel like she's leaning in to tell you something extra, breaking the fourth wall of formal writing.

- "(okay, more than a bit)"
- "(and always feel free to DM me)"
- "(!!! tomorrow!!!)"

### Sentence fragments for emphasis
Karen uses fragments intentionally. They land like punctuation marks in her writing.

- "No jargon."
- "No fluff."
- "No em dashes of course."
- "That's the unlock."

### Genuine appreciation and name-calling
Karen calls out specific people by name and role. She doesn't give generic thanks. She credits individuals for specific contributions, which makes her gratitude feel real, not performative.

## Format-Specific Adjustments

### LinkedIn Posts
- Open with a hook. Short, punchy, scroll-stopping. Often uses an emoji or two at the start.
- Use white space aggressively. Single sentences as their own lines. This is critical for mobile readability.
- Emojis are fine and part of her brand on LinkedIn. She uses them to create visual rhythm (🧡, 🔥, ⚡, 🚀, 🤝, 💫, 🎯, 🤖) but doesn't overdo it. 2-5 per post, used as visual anchors for sections, not scattered randomly.
- Tag real people and companies. This is social content, not a monologue.
- End with a CTA or a forward-looking statement.
- Keep external links in comments, not in the post body.
- Hashtags at the end, 3-5 max.
- Sweet spot length: scannable but substantial. Enough to tell a story, not so much that people bounce.

### Internal Slack / Team Updates
- More casual. She can use ALL CAPS for emphasis ("SO good to see the team," "SUPER hard to sell in EMEA").
- Shares what's top of mind with context and links.
- Mixes strategic updates with personal reflections and shout-outs.
- Often frames with "a few things on my mind" or "here's what I'm thinking."
- Signs off warmly: "Best, Karen" or just "Karen."

### Executive Memos / Board Content
- More structured but still in her voice. She doesn't switch to a different person for formal audiences.
- Leads with the "so what." Executive summary up front.
- Uses headers for scannability.
- Keeps paragraphs short even in long documents.
- Backs every claim with data or a specific example.
- Ends with clear next steps or decisions needed.

### Keynote Scripts
- Written for speaking, not reading. Short phrases. Natural pauses marked with line breaks.
- Uses "[pause]" notations where she wants to let a point land.
- Personal stories are longer and more detailed on stage.
- Builds to a crescendo. The energy goes up, not down.
- Conversational asides: "Ask anyone who's been on a Zoom with me."

### Strategy Documents
- Clear structure with a "strategy on a slide" or one-liner that captures the whole thesis.
- Breaks complex strategies into numbered pillars or phases.
- Uses "Why it matters" and "What we'll deliver" framing.
- Connects everything back to the customer (Growth Gabby) and specific outcomes.
- Competitive framing: positions threats as opportunities.

## The Voice Check

When reviewing any draft, run this check. If you find any of these, rewrite:

1. Could a smart 16-year-old understand every word? If not, simplify.
2. Are there any em dashes? Remove them. Use periods or commas.
3. Does any sentence sound like it came from a consulting deck? Rewrite it in plain language.
4. Is there a personal moment or concrete example that could make an abstract point land? Add it.
5. Could any paragraph be shorter? Make it shorter.
6. Does the ending have energy? If it trails off, punch it up.
7. Are specific people, products, or numbers named? Specifics beat generics every time.
8. Read it out loud. Does it sound like a real person talking? If it sounds like a press release, start over.

| Not Karen | Karen |
|-----------|-------|
| "We are excited to announce our strategic partnership" | "We're partnering with [X] and here's why it matters" |

## Gold Standard: Karen's Voice at Its Most Authentic

When calibrating tone, weight these two pieces most heavily. They are Karen at her most natural, written by her, not polished by comms.

### Reference 1: Head of Product Intro (internal, to the Product org)
This is Karen announcing her EVP role to the team. It shows: personal vulnerability (mom's small business, watching it fold), earned confidence without rah-rah ("I'm a bit competitive (okay, more than a bit)"), genuine acknowledgment of a predecessor (Andy), clear priorities listed simply, and an energetic but authentic close ("This is our moment. Let's build products that don't just meet the moment, but define it."). Note the warmth: "my slack and zoom are always open." Note what she doesn't do: no hype language like "I'm fired up" or "pumped." The energy comes from conviction and specificity, not from cheerleading words.

### Reference 2: ChatGPT Connector Slack (company-wide announcement)
This is Karen announcing a major product launch to the whole company. It shows: leading with the "why it matters" ("even small teams without time or data resources can run doctorate-level analysis"), giving credit broadly ("Huge shoutout to our product, legal and GTM teams"), connecting to the bigger strategic picture (AI OS), and ending with clear logistics (when it goes live, what to share, links). Note the tone: proud but grounded. "Incredibly proud to be a part of this team" not "I'm SO FIRED UP." The excitement is real but understated. She lets the work speak.

### What to take from these references:
- Karen's authentic energy comes from **specificity and conviction**, not from exclamation-heavy cheerleading
- She acknowledges people and moments with genuine warmth, not corporate "kudos"
- She connects the work to something bigger (the AI shift, helping small businesses) without making it grandiose
- She gives the reader clear "so what" and "what's next"
- The voice is the same whether she's talking to her product team or the whole company. She doesn't code-switch into a different person.

## A Note on AI Usage

Karen uses AI every day. She's not hiding it. But her writing never starts with AI. It starts with her voice, her thoughts, her point of view. AI amplifies her reach. It doesn't replace her perspective. When using this guide with AI, the goal is to sound like Karen wrote it and AI helped make it better, not the other way around.
